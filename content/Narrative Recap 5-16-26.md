The wall behind Sir Viltevar had fallen with the speed of an executioner's blade. The pleasant theory that confidence is the greater part of diplomacy survived our hero's entrance into the hallway by approximately one syllable.

[[Shade]], who is the sort of man for whom a sudden tactical reversal is simply a different shape of opportunity, was already in motion. He had taken the wall's force-shove forward, turned the stumble into a roll, and come up with that great new heavy crossbow of his at his shoulder. Three bolts in the time it takes a lesser archer to nock one; two of them found home. (One notes, for the chronicle, that Shade ended the maneuver a healthy distance down the hall, which is to say that he had at least the sense to put space between himself and the thing at the far end.) The thing at the far end did not return the courtesy. It stood, gestured, and spoke a word of magical command; Shade hit the ground. The sentinel had opinions about who should and should not be upright in its hallway.

[[Vyth]], a step behind our hero when the wall had fallen, had felt the same force and hopped clear with the unhurried grace of a man who had seen it coming a half-second sooner than the rest. He spoke a Bless over himself, Shade, and the learned mage. The familiar warmth of it settled upon Sir Viltevar's shoulders, an attention he appreciated more than he would have admitted aloud.

The arcane gentleman attempted, of course, to Dispel the wall. Nothing answered. He could feel the transmutation aura plainly: heavy, settled, finished. It took him a beat longer than he would have liked to recognize what it was telling him. *Stone Shape.* The door had not been enchanted closed; the door had been reshaped closed. There was nothing left to unweave. Stone does not care about the opinions of wizards, however learned. (Through the breach a moment later, our chronicler will note, the noble scribe would see the after-image clearly: the masonry had been pulled across the opening like stretched dough, then released to settle.)

Fortunately, [[Kaldor]] is less elegant than the great wizard and more direct. He spoke Thunderwave at the wall from the outside and blew a hole in it the size of a doorway. The party was reunited.

They were not, however, a safe party. Crossing the threshold into the hallway-room invoked some manner of standing tidal pressure that none of the band had felt upon entry; perhaps the trap had not been live until it fired, perhaps they had simply not been deep enough in to register it before. [[Amos Crane|Amos]] caught it across the knees as he entered and was thrown to the ground hard enough that Sir Viltevar winced for him. Kaldor read the room correctly, declined to rush in, and let his Moonbeam do the moving for him. Shade rolled with another pulse from prone, opened the sentinel's armor with a clean shot, and stood with the momentum of the strike, putting himself between the thing and the rest of the band. There is a particular silent agreement that passes between a wizard and a ranger in moments like that one. The chronicler will not dignify it with words.

Amos strode through the breach, hurled his javelin, and the javelin became a bolt of lightning before it had finished its arc. The crack of it was beautiful. (Our hero makes no apology for noticing.) Amos blinked the distance to the sentinel's flank without a wasted motion (the man's economy of movement remains a wonder to the learned mage) and brought his sword across in the same breath. The strike glanced off armor. The sentinel had not even looked at him.

It was looking at Sir Viltevar.

Our hero had a fraction of a heartbeat to consider whether to be flattered (the sentinel's evident judgment being that he was the dangerous one in the room), and then it stepped over Shade (who got a parting shot in as it passed, as did Amos) and connected. The chronicler should like to report that our valiant hero weathered the blow with dignity. The truth is that he went down hard, and that he would have gone down considerably harder had Vyth not interposed a wall of divine grace at the last instant from across the room. The man spent a piece of himself to keep the learned mage on his feet (well, on his back, technically, but in one piece); Sir Viltevar has not forgotten it. The sentinel followed up with something one had not seen before: it poured a black necrotic energy into a tearing bite of its own armor-plates, and the wound it took healed. The thing was feeding off the room. Our hero filed that away.

At the start of Sir Viltevar's next turn the tidal force slammed him back to the floor again, and Vyth reached for him a second time. The man is, and the chronicler is writing this down so our hero does not forget to say it aloud, a better friend than the noble scribe deserves.

Standing felt like wading through cold honey. The arcane gentleman made it to his feet, called Haste into Amos (the man wears three attacks better than anyone the learned mage has run with), and bared his teeth at the sentinel through what was no doubt a charming amount of blood. Kaldor muscled through the tide and dropped a Moonbeam directly on the thing's head. Shade circled into a gap in the sentinel's plate and drove his dagger home, and watched the dagger find purchase, and watched the blow do nothing. So that was confirmed: immune to ordinary steel. The morning had been educational.

Then Amos hit it with a Smite, and the sentinel lit up. Radiant fire clung to its surface like phosphor on a wet rope, and every attack the band made on it from that moment forward came at advantage. The sentinel, with the unhurried obstinacy of a thing that has been winning fights for a great deal of time, stepped out of the Moonbeam and tried to power up some manner of area effect, and walked itself directly through all of the party's opportunities. Kaldor put a spell through it on the way past. The sentinel staggered, twitched, and dropped.

The band allowed themselves perhaps two breaths of relief before the room reminded them, in the form of the tide pinning the learned mage to the floor again, that the trap was the room and the sentinel was only the door's enforcer. Sir Viltevar held Haste, Misty Stepped out into the broken corridor, and turned his attention to the apparatus in the room: a metal chest at the hallway's end, and the rest of the party closing on it for the off switch.

Kaldor saw it first. An indentation in the chest's lid the exact shape of a book. A slit along the front sized for a blade. The puzzle announced itself plainly. Shade considered his new runed rapier, walked through the tide as though it were not there (whatever discipline he runs on, the room could not find purchase on him), and slid the blade home in the slit.

And our hero felt his pack pull at him from the inside.

Shade called back without looking. *"Throw the story book to me!"* The arcane gentleman muttered, to no one in particular, *"aww, but I love that book,"* and then more publicly, with the warmth of a man yielding gracefully to circumstance, *"well, if you think it will work, I'll get it to you."*

What followed was instructive. The sentinel, broken not three breaths ago, twitching on the floor, stood back up. The room had stitched it together. It rounded on Vyth, who was still slow, and hit him hard, and Vyth answered with a column of necrotic force the learned mage had not realized he had at his disposal and folded the thing back to the floor. So: confirmed. The room mends what the party breaks. The band had a clock.

Amos spotted runes on the ceiling and threw his javelin at them (a fair thought, no effect) and called out the location. *"On it!"* Sir Viltevar called back with the brisk professionalism the moment required, and shrugged his pack off his shoulder, wondering whether the pull on it would let it drag like a fish on a line. The pack hit the floor, the pull did not move it, and in that small failure our hero understood the trick: this was no pull at all, but a teleportation reaching for a specific object, and the object (the story book) warped out of his pack and appeared next to Shade at the chest.

*"Shaaaaddde,"* the arcane gentleman called, with the small wounded dignity of a man whose belongings have been redistributed without his consent, *"I was going to toss it, but it sort of warped away?"*

He then made the arcane motions to wake his own grimoire and sent it floating to the dead center of the room, halfway to the ceiling, a small theatrical gesture our hero had been waiting some weeks to use in earnest. He looked through its pages as though through a window. Through the spellbook he saw the sentinel clearly, and through the spellbook he sent a Chill Touch into it to keep it pinned down while the rest of the party finished the puzzle. The shape of the spell hovered over the thing like a black hand, and the sentinel did not get up again.

Kaldor pulled both books from Sir Viltevar's pack ([[Udious]]'s spellbook, and the story book) and held them out for Shade to compare against the indentation. The story book matched. Shade set it in. The chest drank the book and the rapier together, lifted itself off the floor, and began to drift unhurriedly around the room. The runes on the ceiling dimmed. The chest tipped forward in the air, and opened.

The session took an intermission.

When the chronicle resumed: the chest settled to the center of the room. Its lid rose to the ceiling and stayed there; its front face fell open like a tongue. Inside, a pile of gold and gems, and resting atop the pile, an **orb.** A globe of dark stone marked across its surface with swirls and lines like ley lines on a map, radiating, plain as daylight to the learned mage's Arcana, both abjuration and divination. And the name surfaced in our hero's mind whole, as such things sometimes do in places like this: the **[[Abracadabrus and Astromancy Archive]].**

Sir Viltevar tried, naturally, to open it. It declined. He will return to it. He will return to it more than once.

The coinage was (and this is the part that lit the room for the noble scribe) every coin stamped with a **pre-Kingdom [[Keoland History|Keoland]] mark.** Older than the current state. Older than the institutions one currently calls Keoland. These coins predate things that are themselves considered ancient in any merchant's strongbox today. The band pocketed them. The arcane gentleman will need to be careful about where he spends them; the wrong gold in the wrong palm is a question one does not need to be answering.

The party agreed, over the closing of the chest behind them, that this room (once they figure out how to switch the trap off) would be the vault. Whatever the band lays their hands on of value goes here. The Inn at [[Crabber's Cove]] remains first priority for restoration. This is the second priority. A vault inside a tower inside a cove. The shape of an adventuring life is beginning to feel, to the noble of high lineage, like the shape of a household.

The band took a short rest in the hexagonal room, banter and watered wine and a smoke for those who do. Sir Viltevar made another attempt on the orb. The orb continued to disagree with him.

Then the party elected Shade to take point on the south door (Room 4), and he drew his weapon and pushed it open.

The room behind it was a **grove.**

A real one. Living trees, branches breathing slowly in some interior weather of their own; the smell of leaf-mould and warm earth; light filtering down through a canopy that should not have been able to fit inside a tower basement. Between the doorway and the grove proper hung a faint membrane, perceptible more to the skin than to the eye. They stepped through. The membrane did not resist.

Amos caught a moment as he crossed, the look of a man who has walked, by accident, into his grandmother's kitchen mid-afternoon, with fresh bread on the table and an autumn wind moving through the open window. He blinked, and the moment passed, and he was a paladin again.

Shade asked aloud whether the whole thing might be an illusion. Sir Viltevar leaned against the nearest trunk to check. The bark was bark. The leaves were leaves. Whatever this room was, it was not a painted backdrop.

Shade spotted, after a moment of scanning, a pattern of trampled growth in the grass that resembled the destination side of a teleport circle. Our hero noted the location and said nothing aloud about it.

The animals were the second strangeness. Rich variety, more than the space should sustain, and Vyth flagged it: he had never seen some of these creatures before. The arcane gentleman is inclined to believe him; the man's catalog is wider than the learned mage's own. Amos (bless him) fell to talking to a two-tailed squirrel, who informed him, in the matter-of-fact way of small woodland creatures who are used to being asked obvious questions, that the band stood in the **Grove of [[Obad-Hai]].**

The chronicler will admit, on these pages alone, that our hero was enthralled. The grove caught him on some level he had not been guarding. He did not move for a moment. He noticed that he had not moved. He made no apology for either fact.

Shade, more practical, began to look for the way out. A voice answered him, pleasant, unhurried, amused.

*"Leaving so soon?"*

A man stepped into the band's awareness as though he had simply decided to be visible. Middle years, druidic garb, a **bullfrog perched on his shoulder** with the patient dignity of a courtier. He introduced himself: **[[Ferrin Kastilar]].** *"I'm glad to have visitors to my grove."*

The chronicler will pick up this account where it left off, and will note (for the record, and because the recording book demands accuracy) that Sir Viltevar entered the grove of a god he had not yet introduced himself to, and was greeted not as an intruder but as **a guest.**

One shall see what is asked of the guest in return.
