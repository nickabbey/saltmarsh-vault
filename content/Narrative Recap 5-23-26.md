[[Ferrin Kastilar|Ferrin]], as it turns out, is a gracious host. He led the party out of the grove proper and into a smaller clearing, a still place with a pond at its center, and made them tea with the unhurried gestures of a man who has had a long time to learn how. He chatted easily with [[Amos Crane|Amos]] as he worked; the bullfrog on his shoulder (whose name, our hero would learn shortly, is **Lorys**) observed the proceedings with an air of grave senatorial dignity. The band sat. They drank. Some part of Sir Viltevar (the chronicler permits the admission, on these pages alone) had relaxed completely; the noble scribe had allowed the place its quiet; the learned mage had been beguiled, very gently, by good tea and the smell of leaf-mould.

Then Ferrin set down his cup and asked Amos about the tenets of his oath.

Our hero had heard [[Amos Crane|Amos]] speak on this subject before. The man has a refrain he returns to, neither rehearsed nor reluctant. It amounts to: *let nature take its course; sometimes that course is violence.* He said as much, in essentially those words. The arcane gentleman does not know, even now, whether Amos gave the wrong answer or the right one and merely the wrong audience. One does not know whether Ferrin asked the question on his own behalf, or on behalf of his god, or on behalf of the grove itself (which our hero is beginning to suspect may have an opinion). What the learned mage knows is that the moment shifted. The light did not change; the air did not move; but something in the quiet of the place became attentive.

And then everyone the band loved began to become something else.

Sir Viltevar's awakened grimoire, which had been resting beside him with the comfortable warmth of a familiar mind in a borrowed body, lengthened, and darkened, and stood up on a great many legs. The wizard's weasel, who had been his familiar for longer than the chronicler is willing to commit to ink, grew, and went on growing, and was abruptly a sabretoothed tiger, with the weasel's face arranged across its skull in a configuration our hero does not wish to recount. Around the clearing other companions followed suit: a giant constrictor where one familiar had been, a mammoth where another had been, a giant spider rearing beside Sir Viltevar where his book had been a moment before. And [[Kaldor]], who had been lazing in the pond with his usual contemplative buoyancy, was the pond, in some sense; the pond bulged, and then there was a great spotted pufferfish in it instead, with Kaldor's familiar squint.

Ferrin and Lorys faded out of the clearing as politely as they had arrived. The learned mage noted this with the small reflexive part of his mind that notes such things even when the larger part is otherwise engaged.

Now. On these pages alone, the chronicler will admit to a small lapse in judgment on our hero's part. The tiger was enormous. It was, in some clearly nominal sense, still his. It was also (one notes for the record) the most beautiful animal Sir Viltevar has ever stood beside. The thought occurred to him (and the noble of high lineage will not pretend it did not) that astride such a beast he should cut a truly magnificent figure; the kind of figure a chronicler (let us say, hypothetically, a recording book) might find worth recording. Our hero attempted, with what he considered a perfectly reasonable degree of grace, to mount the tiger.

The tiger, it turned out, was not in a mood to be ridden.

The arcane gentleman learned in the next breath that nature is fickle, that beauty is not friendship, and that the sabretoothed face of one's former familiar does not soften when one reaches for the saddle of its shoulders. He Misty Stepped clear. He tried to move further from the cluster of teeth and chitin behind him, lateral and away. The forest closed. Branches that had not been there a moment before were there now, and stayed there, and would not let him pass. So the grove had opinions about the field of battle as well. The learned mage dodged, set his feet, and prepared for what was almost certainly going to be unpleasant.

It was an owlbear.

It came down on Sir Viltevar from above with the casual physics of a great deal of muscle arriving at its destination, and our hero caught the flash of its claws an instant before they would have parted him from his head. *Shield.* The disc of force flared up between them. The owlbear made a sound the chronicler will not attempt to describe in writing. The claws scored the air a hand's breadth from the noble scribe's face, and did not reach it. (One will like the record to reflect that the wizard did not flinch. The wizard almost did not flinch. The wizard's eyebrows committed to an attitude one would have preferred them not to commit to. Magnificent restraint by the wizard's eyebrows under the circumstances.)

[[Shade]] had gone hostile, which is to say *the transformation that did not transform him* had nonetheless drawn him in; the constrictor was already on him, and had him restrained and grappled with terrible efficiency. Amos charged the giant spider that had been our hero's book and landed a crit on it; the spider folded, and in the same instant something at the back of Sir Viltevar's mind went silent. The arcane gentleman had not realized how loud the arcane presence of the grimoire had been until it was not there anymore. The absence rang. Kaldor (insofar as the pufferfish was Kaldor) expanded two full size categories, until he filled a fifteen-foot square on each side, an architectural fish of considerable opinion. [[Vyth]], restrained and miserable in the snake's coils, was nonetheless functioning; the learned mage would not realize until later that this is essentially Vyth's default condition.

At the top of the next round, Shade broke free of the snake, and Kaldor changed again, this time into some long-necked creature with a twenty-foot reach. Kaldor (in this new form) bolted past Sir Viltevar, and the chronicler will note with envy that he made it considerably further into the trees than our hero had managed; either the grove was being lenient with him for reasons of its own, or each of them was working against a different boundary. Long-necked Kaldor struck at Amos from twenty feet of neck and hurt him badly. Sir Viltevar circled around the owlbear (which is a sentence the noble scribe would not have predicted writing that morning) and dropped a **Fireball** between the mammoth and the constrictor. The mammoth, which had until that moment been a distant problem, decided he was a closer one; it charged, it gored him, and the learned mage went to the ground. It reared to bring its full weight down through his chest, and somehow did not. Whether by the grove's grace or by our hero's own poor footing carrying him a hand's breadth out of the killing line, one cannot say. The arcane gentleman will take it.

Vyth, restrained and still swinging, missed badly at Kaldor with steel, and then conjured a **Spiritual Weapon** that did not miss; it struck the long-necked thing with a satisfying crack of force. Amos turned his attention to the spider that had been the learned mage's book. The round ended.

Kaldor changed again. A huge crab this time. The chronicler has run with Kaldor for some while and confesses he has never seen the man so industrious in the matter of self-revision. Amos, in the chaos of the transition, broke free and landed on his feet. Shade tried to taunt the mammoth into following him; the mammoth declined the invitation, but Shade cut a great quantity out of it on the way and kept taunting from the safer distance. Sir Viltevar stood. He moved south. He cast his second **Fireball** of the engagement directly between the owlbear and the mammoth, and watched them both burn, and watched the mammoth take damage that should by every accounting have ended it.

The mammoth fully rejuvenated.

The chronicler will not pretend our hero took this well. He cursed, in Elvish, and very quietly. He gritted his teeth against the realization that the band had been fighting a trap; that there was no kill condition in the room; that anyone who fell would simply be in worse shape than when the round started. (Beware, our chronicler observes for posterity, the encounter in which one's opponents heal completely on death; that is not a fight, it is a sermon.)

Kaldor shrieked. The grove went briefly very loud. One believes the intent was for the band to scatter in terror. They did not scatter. They are, on these pages alone, exactly stupid enough not to. The owlbear took the opportunity to put its claws through our hero. The learned mage burned a **Silvery Barbs** in panic, redirecting the worst of it, and gave the rebound to himself, hoping advantage on his next save would mean anything. It did not. The mammoth gored him a second time, and the math was unkind: twenty-three points past his last remaining one, and the world went dim, and Sir Viltevar went down.

One is told (after the fact, with the appropriately dramatic embellishments) that Vyth, out of spells and out of patience, made a desperate weapon attack on Kaldor in his most recent and most architecturally improbable form; that the rest of the party hung on by their teeth; that there was a moment when the entire band was within a breath of failing the test, whatever the test was. Our hero cannot speak to it directly. He was, on these pages alone, *unconscious,* which is an embarrassing thing for a wizard to be on the record about. The record has a duty to truth.

Sir Viltevar came back to the room at one hit point. He was looking at the canopy. [[Ferrin Kastilar|Ferrin]] was standing over him, hands folded, with Lorys back on his shoulder, beaming in the way that only certain old men can beam.

*"Wasn't that fun?"* he said.

The arcane gentleman blinked at him. He had no immediate riposte. (On these pages alone: this is a rare condition for the learned mage, and the chronicler records it for completeness.)

Amos lifted our hero with the matter-of-fact grace he applies to most physical labor and carried him to the pond at Ferrin's prompting. The water closed around them both, and Sir Viltevar's wounds knit, and Amos's did the same. In the same moment the silence at the back of the learned mage's mind ended; the **awakened grimoire** was there again, the familiar weight of a borrowed mind settling back into place, slightly indignant at having been briefly dead. Our hero has not said this aloud to it. He will not. It would only encourage the indignation.

Ferrin explained. *"This was a test. The followers of [[Obad-Hai]] in this group have potential. I needed to draw it out."* He claimed, with the bland confidence of a man who could have ended the entire party at any time during the proceedings, that the band had not been in any real danger. The noble scribe considered, for a moment, several possible responses. He chose the better part of valor and said nothing. A man who can heal a fatal goring with a dip in his pond does not need to hear our hero's opinions on his definition of *danger.* (On these pages alone: it did not feel un-dangerous.)

Ferrin turned, then, to the orb.

The **[[Abracadabrus and Astromancy Archive]]**, which had ridden in Sir Viltevar's pack and refused to open for him on three separate occasions since [[Crabber's Cove]], opened for Ferrin with the unhurried obedience of a thing recognizing the right hand. He showed our hero how. The chronicler will not record the gesture here, partly because the learned mage wishes to keep the knowledge between Ferrin and himself, and partly because the doing of it is not a thing the page can hold. What the recording book will record is the moment after: a heads-up display blooming inside Sir Viltevar's mind, a configuration of light he had not had room for an hour before, **spells aligned to celestial spheres.** Augury. Divination. Find the Path. Foresight. Locate Creature. Locate Object. The full divination wardrobe of a serious wizard, laid out across rings of light like notes on a stave. It functions as a spellbook. It functions as a focus. It carries charges, and the charges do things the arcane gentleman is very much looking forward to using in earnest. (Including, our hero notes with a thrill the chronicler will not pretend is dignified, the ability to make a creature's roll worse with the flick of an attention.)

And it carries a **journal.**

[[Udious]]'s journal.

The chronicler will not transcribe the whole of it here. Our hero is, in honesty, still reading it. The bones of the story are these. Udious (whose full name, the journal tells him, was *Udious Odiuous*; the learned mage will leave the obvious commentary to the reader) and his party retired here, to this tower, after their adventuring days. They found a tomb, and in the tomb was the vampire [[Xoltec]], who came from across the sea, and who was mind-controlling local priests in the region. One of those priests was a friend of the party. There was a fight. A companion died. One priest escaped, taking a different companion's weapon with him. From that day, Udious devoted himself to ending Xoltec. He sold a piece of his party's history to [[Keoland History|Keoland]] to fund the building of this tower as his workshop; he cloned the vampire (cloned the vampire) to dissect its strengths and weaknesses in conditions of safety; he leaned heavily on the druids of the Sea Grove for daylight spells (*"He's talking about us!"* Ferrin interjected, with the warmth of a man at the edge of fond memory). The journal grows less careful as it proceeds. Late entries speak of *fast and loose* methods. Tests that went sideways. A final experiment he believed would close the matter for good. He worried, in those late pages, for his own mind, but trusted his companions to keep him stable.

He never names them. Not once. Sir Viltevar notices this. The arcane gentleman does not yet know what to make of it.

Shade showed Ferrin his runed rapier, asking whether it matched anything in the story. Ferrin turned the weapon in his hands and pointed out, very gently, that the runes along its blade *are the story of how Udious died.* The chronicler cannot improve on that sentence by appending to it. Shade asked after the weapon sold to Keoland; Ferrin knew only that it had gone there, and not where in the kingdom it had ended up.

Amos produced a shield he had been carrying and asked Ferrin about it. Ferrin demurred politely (not his field) but observed, with the patience of a man making a point he expected to be remembered, that a *dedicated servant of [[Obad-Hai]]* ought to be carrying gear that had been *blessed by [[Obad-Hai]].* He pressed into Amos's hands a **leaf-shaped sword** that drank the light of the clearing and held it, and an **autumn cloak** that moved as though carrying its own faint wind. Amos accepted both with the quiet dignity he applies to most ceremonies. Then he turned, with that same dignity, and offered Sir Viltevar his **old Cloak of Protection.** *I have the new one now,* he said, as if it were the simplest fact in the world. Our hero draped it across his shoulders without ceremony. (On these pages alone: the noble scribe felt the difference immediately. The cloak knows its work.)

Ferrin offered to supply Kaldor with ingredients for half a dozen potions; the band selected, with the giddy unanimity of a party that has not yet had to pay full ingredient prices, *rares.* Kaldor will still have to spend the time at his bench, but the hunting and haggling are done.

Then, the merge.

Ferrin showed the learned mage how to fold his awakened grimoire into the archive itself. The book passed into the sphere as though through still water; the archive kept what it had been (a focus, a divination instrument, a holder of charges) and gained what the grimoire had been: a focus in its own right, a quickener of attack and save against the noble scribe's casting, a companion presence our hero had grown so used to that he had forgotten how warm it was until it settled into the orb's frame. Every spell Sir Viltevar had transcribed in his career was inside the archive when the merge resolved. One container. One focus. One mind, intact.

The arcane gentleman attempted, on the strength of the moment, to fold in Udious's spellbook as well. The archive declined. Politely, but firmly. Udious's book remains in our hero's pack, a separate physical volume, and any of his spells the learned mage wants in the archive he will have to write onto its surface the way he would write onto any other book: time, ink, and components.

Which is, of course, what the noble scribe excused himself to do.

The party came back to the tower. Most of the band turned around immediately and headed back into town to find masons and carpenters; this place is theirs now, after a fashion, and it needs to look like it. Sir Viltevar reminded them (with what he hoped was the brisk efficiency of a man who has not just been gored to death by a mammoth) to check in with the [[Saltmarsh Town Council|Council]] and with [[Gellan Primewater|Primewater]] while they were among the public. (Visibility is a tool. The learned mage has been turning it over in his hands a great deal lately.) Then our hero took a room, and he took a bottle of decent wine that someone had liberated from somewhere, and he sat down with [[Udious]]'s spellbook in one hand and the open archive in the other, and he started writing.

There is a particular pleasure in adding another wizard's life's work to one's own collection. It is not exactly grief, and it is not exactly inheritance. It is closest, the chronicler suspects, to the feeling of finishing a book and immediately starting to copy the better passages into one's commonplace. The dead man's hand is on the page; one corrects his minor errors as one goes; one lets his cleverness become one's own. Sir Viltevar, working by the soft light of a tower window, transcribing a vampire-hunter's spells into a stolen god's astrolabe, cut (on these pages alone) a figure the chronicler would very much like to have been able to step outside and *see.*

Magnificent.
