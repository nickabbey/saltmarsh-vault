The recording book passed into Sir Viltevar's keeping by way of [[Shade]], who had pressed it into his hands at the start of the day with the offhand air of a man giving up something he no longer wants. The learned mage accepted the gift in the spirit it must surely have been intended, and which he would not embarrass the giver by examining too closely. The tome itself he had wanted for some time. It records what is spoken into it. Our valiant chronicler would speak into it everything that mattered. Posterity, he reflected with the equanimity of a man long since accustomed to its expectations of him, would thank him.

[[Crabber's Cove]] first. The magistrate met the party with the deed in hand. A few of the others bent over the parchment and read it line by line; our noble wizard, recognizing where the moment's true significance lay, attended to the flourish of his signature: a long downstroke, a gentle curl, a final dot placed with a small ceremonial pause. The magistrate did not visibly weep, but she was, in his judgment, moved.

Over the unsigned ash of what had once been a roof, the [[Saltmarsh Renegades|Renegades]] talked priorities. They were fresh from a tournament and disinclined to anything strenuous. His Lordship had wanted the tower (which had, after all, the more interesting problem set), but accepted the sense in beginning with the largest of the four buildings (the future Inn) and making it livable first. The party cleared debris, stacked the wreckage, and agreed to set the pile ablaze at sundown to mark their first night on the property.

Sir Viltevar inquired, in passing, after the vampire. The party shrugged, rubbed at their necks, and explained it was a long story but he had left. Our hero filed the answer where he files most answers he does not believe, and returned to the work. He would learn, before the day was out, that he had been right to.

[[Shade]] and the valiant Viltevar caught crabs by the bonfire. [[Shade]] demonstrated the trick of it with the easy patience of a man who has been reading shallow water since before he had a name for it; the wizard caught a few of the smaller ones. He observed aloud that the party ought to commission proper traps from a carpenter and put crab on the menu of the Inn: passive food for the table, profit on the side, and the kind of small gracious touch that turns a tavern into an institution. The party was generally amenable, though more for the food than the institution. This is the way of things, and the noble of high lineage has long since accepted his obligation to think a few steps further on for those who do not.

Before sunrise, [[Shade]] knocked at his door. The two of them ran the brush and the shallows together, and returned in the grey of dawn for [[Shade]] to demonstrate the basics of his kata. The form was unexpectedly familiar to our elegant elf. Whatever lineage it descended from, it touched near the same root as the discipline of his family's man-at-arms, who had drilled him in the practice yard as a boy. Sir Viltevar has rarely been moved by a piece of exercise. He was, briefly, that morning.

Over breakfast they talked the property again. The learned mage floated the obvious plan: approach [[Gellan Primewater]] and barter the Renegades' adventuring services for guild labor. Whatever spoils were won on the work Primewater sent them, the party would keep; Primewater would pay in carpenters and stonemasons against the buildings. The reception was mixed (the others lacked, on the morning in question, the patience for mercantile strategy at full breakfast volume), but the smaller version carried: crab traps commissioned at the soonest opportunity. A start.

[[Amos Crane|Amos]] led the party to the tower. Down through the main chamber and into the basement: a hexagonal room, six steel doors around its perimeter, three of them faintly humming with old ward-magic. Sir Viltevar took the center, cast Detect Magic, and called the auras out as he read them: one necromancy, two abjuration. [[Perrin]] steadied his mind as he worked the Arcana check on the necromancy aura (the man has the kind of quiet competence that turns an Arcana check into a partnership). The diagnosis was unambiguous: a chronomancy effect of unpleasant elegance, *Tether Essence,* which binds two creatures within range at random and shunts the injuries of one onto the other. A small cruelty laid into stonework, waiting decades for someone to step inside.

The arcane gentleman tried for a clean dispel and failed; the ward fired. The strands reached for him with that cold inevitability the chronomantic schools wear so well, and slipped past, finding nothing they could hold to. A near thing. Our hero will not, in these pages, dwell on how near.

The two abjuration wards he unwrote one after the other. Symbol spells, both of them: one would have lit the ceiling with antimagic; the other he could not fully read before he had it apart, only that it was bound for the floor. He siphoned the weave from each before it could complete and let the energy dissipate harmlessly into the air.

[[Shade]] and [[Perrin]] bent to the locks. Two doors swung open: one along the southern arc, and another to the southwest. The remaining locked doors stayed shut for the moment. The party agreed (on the wizard's recommendation) to take the two newly accessible rooms first, on the simple logic that opening more might wake more.

The first room held the answer to our hero's morning question.

A row of glass tubes stood along its wall, each filled with a clear suspending fluid, each holding a body. Hags. [[Sahuagin]]. Beasts no one could name on first sight. Creatures of categories the learned mage would not have thought to keep on the same shelf. The runework at the base of each tube read as a binding closer to stasis than imprisonment (somewhere between Gentle Repose and Feign Death), modified beyond anything he had ever seen written down. Whether the modifications were this region's idea or someone else's, he could not say. The tops of the tubes carried abjuration; the bodies inside carried necromancy; and the bases (flush with the floor) looked, to a careful eye, like locked trapdoors.

There was a desk. There were no papers on it. There were no papers anywhere in the room.

And then, in one of the tubes along the wall, suspended in the fluid as though arranged for display: the vampire. The very one the party had assured Sir Viltevar had *left.* [[Amos Crane|Amos]] saw him first and gasped. He had not left. He had never left. He had been placed.

The party exited and locked the door behind them.

The door to the north opened on a tunnel: wood-braced, dug rather than carved, running farther than the elven eye could follow. The party agreed to come back to it.

The other newly opened door gave on something rarer.

The chamber resonated: walls, ceiling, and a half dozen crystalline platforms set at intervals across the floor, all of them humming in the same key. Each platform bore a label in a different tongue, each labeling a creature type: humanoid in Common nearest the door, then Dragon in Draconic, Elemental in Primordial, Aberration in Deep Speech, Beast in Druidic, Celestial in Celestial, Undead in Undercommon, Fey in Sylvan. The valiant Viltevar touched Fey. Nothing answered. [[Amos Crane|Amos]] touched Fey, and the room became a vision: a floating eye's perspective, gentle as a spell of *Scrying,* looking on a dryad [[Amos Crane|Amos]] had once known. He held the moment longer than he needed to. (Our chronicler would note, for the record, that the man has a tenderness about old friendships which the rest of his line of work has not managed to weather out of him. It is one of his finer qualities.)

After the vision faded, the Fey crystal's glow had visibly dimmed. Limited charges, then; not endless use. Our hero stepped to the Undead platform and tested it on the vampire just sealed away. The chamber bent itself into an uncanny copy of the room he had walked out of, the tubes and the bodies and the dust faithfully rendered, and the wizard stood for a moment among his own footprints from a quarter hour ago.

A scrying chamber that needs only a creature type and a speaker of the right language to look anywhere in the world that creature might be found. The learned mage observed aloud that the chamber would have immediate utility in the coming war against the [[Sahuagin]]. No one disagreed.

The party chose the door that had carried the necromancy ward next. The doors that wear the worst spells, in the noble scribe's accumulated experience, tend to hide the things worth finding.

Before they reached it, [[Perrin]] spoke. He had received a summons, he said. None of the rest of the party had heard anything. He was firm: it was time for him to leave. He pressed his Ring of Spell Storing into [[Shade]]'s hand without ceremony and without a backward look (a gesture our chronicler will not pretend was not magnificently performed; the man has a way of leaving a room). The timing was strange. [[Perrin]] had spoken not long after the *Tether Essence* had reached for Sir Viltevar and failed; our hero was, and would continue to be, turning the coincidence over. Whether the two were linked he did not yet know. In time, he reflected, he would find out.

Beyond the door: a long hallway-like room. At the far end sat a large stone-like figure, its hands resting on the lid of a metal chest or strongbox. Sir Viltevar has always believed that confidence is the better part of diplomacy, and so he strode forward at the head of the party with his hand raised in friendly greeting.

*"Ahoy, mate!"* he called.

A click sounded at his feet. A wall fell behind him, cleanly cutting off those who had been less eager. The stone figure ahead unfolded itself slowly to its feet. It had no intention whatsoever of returning the greeting.

Our valiant hero will pick up this account where it left off, in the hope that he is the one writing it.
