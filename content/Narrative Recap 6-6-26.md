The campaign had entered a quieter season; a fortnight's grace before the sahuagin would face their reckoning. Our intrepid hero greeted it as any man of action greets a lull between battles: with industry. There remained, as there always remains in a place worth holding, a corner left unexamined. [[Shade]] had a mind to examine it. He proposed a descent into the tunnel the band had left unexplored beneath the lower levels, and Sir Viltevar, who holds that a knight does not send a companion into the dark alone, went with him, while the faithful [[Amos Crane|Amos]] made their number three. His broad shoulders would prove, before the hour was out, the very foundation of the enterprise. (One records, for the benefit of posterity, that the learned mage's company is its own reward; such esteemed fellows as Amos and Shade ken this by raw intuition, as evidenced by their lack of commentary upon that which is understood amongst fellows.) 

The heroic trio wandered for hours,  a pervasive sense of losing their bearings making it hard to tell how sprawling the coastal caves were. Muddy and teeming with verminous life, the Renegades three came across a vertical shaft with a ladder extending down into the tunnel with a scattering of broken marble at its foot; conspicuously incongruous debris, indeed. The party peered up into the darkness of this sturdily wrought ladder, seeing a sheer marble shaft rising into the dark above. The noble scribe, as is his way, asked the air a question before trusting it: a magical interrogation that, in the primitive languages of mortals might be named **Detect Magic.** The dark answered. An **abjuration aura** clung to the place, the particular signature of a ward built to keep things out rather than to harm the curious. Having judged the danger and found it wanting, our hero advised Shade that he might look closer, safely... _*PROBABLY*_. Which is precisely the degree of certainty a wise man offers a friend before a locked door.

It was, in fact, a lock, perched high, in a cramped vault between the ladder's head and the door above, past the easy reach of any one man climbing alone. The solution the band arrived at was the sort that does not survive into the songs: they lashed Shade to Amos with a good length of rope, and the paladin bore the rogue up the ladder upon his back, as sure footed as a billy-goat, but infinitely more agreeable, until Shade's clever fingers could at last find the tumblers. And then the lock gave Shade rather more trouble than he had bargained for. The arcane gentleman occupied the interval in the manner most natural to him: he thought. He considered the tumblers, the small mechanical honesties on which all locks depend, and he turned over in his mind the several ways a wizard might marry magic to mechanism - especially how one might build a tumbler that *resisted* the picking, that pushed back against the intruding pin. By the time the lock surrendered to Shade's patience, the learned mage had come away with the superior prize: an INSPIRED idea. (Shade had come away with an open door. The chronicler leaves the reader to weigh which of the two will prove the more valuable in the fullness of time.)

Up our intrepid trio climbed, into a **mausoleum** of not insignificant finery. The warmth of a shaft of honest-to-Ehlonna **sunlight** felt as warm as the goddess' touch itself upon our put-upon hero's put-upon brow after what had felt an interminable age of endless subterranean creek beds and verminous muck. Adding to their good fortune, the sunbeam permitted Shade to reckon the band's orientation upon the compass rose. A small competence, cleanly executed, and the chronicler is pleased to know his compatriots are skilled in ways that make The Saltmarsh Renegades one of the greatest groups to take upon themselves the endeavor of the adventurer's life. Within stood a single crypt, wrought in ivory and marble, gilded inlays, and beneath their feet a marvel: a **floor of marble wrought as a dial,** inscribed all round with names, its center the very aperture through which they had emerged. The opening sealed itself behind them after a polite interval, as such things do when they have been built by someone who understood discretion. The dial answered to a touch, turning a single click to the left, and Sir Viltevar - whose memory is a vault that does not leak - **noted the name that opened the way.** Shade set his shoulder to the crypt's door and pushed, and the daylight that spilled in was the ordinary daylight of the **Saltmarsh cemetery.** The door would open no further; a chain on the far side, unreachable from within, saw to that.

Our hero understood at once what he was looking at. A passage that runs unseen from the heart of a man's holdings upon a private cove, to a quiet plot of consecrated ground, sealed from the public side, opening only to one who knows the name - this is not a tomb. This is a **smuggling route,** and a beautiful one. The noble scribe confesses (on these pages alone) that he could not keep the smile from his face. Here was a thoroughfare for goods that prefer to travel without the kingdom's notice; here was the seed of a *privateering concern* of real promise; here was the kind of opportunity that a family unjustly impoverished by a grasping crown might regard as no less than its due. Amos, marking the smile, asked what so pleased him. Our hero, who knows the value of a thing held close, was at first discreet. But Shade joined in and asked again - and a gentleman, pressed twice over, may share his good fortune in the spirit of partnership. So Sir Viltevar told them plainly what he saw.

Shade did not receive it in that spirit. He declared that the passage would serve as an *exit* and nothing more - and then, compounding the matter past all bearing, he presumed to *order* Sir Viltevar to that effect. The chronicler must here lay down the quill a moment and observe, with what restraint he can muster, the magnitude of the presumption. A knight of ancient lineage, a scholar of the higher mysteries, a man whose right to lead is written into his very blood - to be *commanded,* on a question of policy, by a creature of evidently humbler origins than our hero had been led to credit. The learned mage saw red. He answered the presumption as it deserved, with a frank appraisal of not only Shade's station, but his stature, and put the presumptuous little fellow in what was so plainly his place.

What followed, the record must hold faithfully, for the record has a duty to truth even when truth is undignified. Shade, declining the lesson, swept our hero's feet from under him and sprang upon his chest - an indignity the noble scribe does not intend to forget. But a wizard is not so easily kept down. Sir Viltevar seized the offending ankle and twisted as he rolled; Shade hopped clear with the low quickness of his kind and landed light; and the arcane gentleman regained his feet and unfurled his **whip** in a single motion that the chronicler, who saw it, will describe as *effortless,* because it was. He laid the lash across Shade's brow and drove a clap of **thunderous magic** into the blow - *Booming Blade,* in the dry vocabulary of the schools - and instructed the man, plainly, in the true order of things: that he was in no condition to give Sir Viltevar orders, and that he most assuredly did not speak for the [[Saltmarsh Renegades|Renegades]]. Then our hero stepped back, deliberately, *daring* the fuming peasant to make something further of it.

It was at this juncture that [[Amos Crane|Amos]] - who had been present for the whole of it, and might therefore have been expected to recount the sequence of events with some accuracy - stepped in to part them. And here the chronicler's patience is tested a second time. For Amos, surveying a scene in which Shade had given an order he had no standing to give *and* unilaterally dictated the use of a shared asset on behalf of the whole band, somehow contrived to chide *Sir Viltevar* - and on the very charge of presuming to speak for the Renegades, when all our hero had done was *answer a question put to him. Twice.* One marvels, sometimes, at the distribution of blame among well-meaning men. The noble scribe did not dignify the inversion with argument. He kept his own counsel - a discipline he is cultivating with some success - turned the dial, opened the way, and made his exit with what dignity the morning had left him. Encountering [[Vyth]] on the road back to the tower, our hero paused to compose himself: a tug at the doublet, a smoothing of the cloth, a breath blown through disheveled hair. A leader does not let an ill humor spill onto those of better sense than the company he has just left. (Vyth, for his part, said nothing, which is one among Vyth's many fine qualities.)

While the learned mage had been improving Shade's education below, the rest of the band had been about the town's business - and the chronicle, being omniscient, may follow them. [[Kaldor]] had gone hunting carpenters, and had run to ground the **Carpenter's and Mason's Guilds,** engaging them to shore the tower's foundations against the tides, mend its walls, and furnish it properly; he laid down a deposit and the attendant filings, a matter of a few coins, and the band would weigh the bids on the morrow. [[Amos Crane|Amos]] had repaired to **The Wicker Goat,** where the town's guardsmen drink away their shifts, and had set about proving - by the time-honored method of out-drinking dwarves - that he was a man worth teaching the **arts of the shield.** One does not begrudge a paladin his methods. They are as consistent as they are effective.

The second day brought a hatching. [[Amos Crane|Amos]] drew out the **[[Thousand Teeth the Devourer|Thousand Teeth]] egg** to take the air, and the air proved more than the egg could resist; it shook, and rattled, and split, and Amos bore the thing off in haste to be imprinted upon in private. He returned hours later to report that he had introduced the hatchling - a baby crocodile-monster of unwholesome appetite and uncertain future - to **Malaki,** and that the noble beast had consented, with a forbearance the chronicler finds frankly saintly, to play *nursemaid* to the creature. The band received this news as one receives most news at Amos's table: with affection, and without inquiring too closely.

The greater labor of the day, and the one nearest our hero's heart, was the disposition of [[Crabber's Cove|the cove]] itself - for the holding has begun, under the campaign's newer customs, to take the shape of a proper **bastion.** The band laid out its intentions: the second building would become a **crab shack;** the third remained an open question; the tunnels would house a **menagerie;** and the place already offered an **arcane study,** a **laboratory,** and that menagerie, wanting only the right hands to staff them, alongside an **observatory** and a **sanctuary** that wanted repair before they could give anything at all. Sir Viltevar gave the matter the strategic attention it deserved, and his counsel was characteristically clear: of the two damaged rooms the **observatory** is plainly the worthier, the charms it grants outweighing the sanctuary's by any honest reckoning - yet *neither* is worth a copper's investment until the band has restored the **defenses** they themselves disabled and shattered in the taking of the place. First the walls, then the wonders. (The Renegades, wisely, did not dispute the order of operations. Our hero noted, with quiet satisfaction, that they are learning.) He spent the daylight reviewing the contracts the guildsmen brought as they came.

The morning had produced a bid our hero received with rather less warmth: a crew whose spokesman - a gravel-throated fellow with a gold tooth and the unhurried, ingratiating manner of a man who has been politely *encouraged to move along* from a town or two before this one - inspired in the noble scribe a confidence best described as negative. One does not need a divination to read certain faces. But the afternoon redeemed the ledger. One bid pleased him beyond the rest, and the chronicler will not pretend the pleasure was wholly fiscal. A **dwarvish family** offered to carry out the whole of the work through the Carpenter's Guild *without recourse to the mining company* - and the mining company, the reader should understand, is a tendril of **[[Keoland History|Keoghland]],** that nest of avaricious whales whose taxes beggared houses better than theirs. Any commerce our hero may lawfully deny those grasping interests is commerce he conducts with a song in his heart. The bid leapt, on that ground alone, to the head of the line. Meanwhile [[Vyth]] had been dispatched to the **magical market** with the band's collective wishlist; he returned with the familiar tale of such places - wonders beyond their purse, a wealth of honest commons and uncommons within it, and the prospect that each of them might, with care, afford a single **rare.** The market refreshes its stock by some daily caprice, which makes a habit of visiting it merely prudent. Vyth shopped on the band's behalf, and most came away the richer; our hero, for his part, came away with **four new spells** - and the chronicler notes, with the quiet satisfaction of a man whose mind runs in elegant circles, that two of them were **Arcane Lock** and **Knock:** the very marriage of magic and mechanism Sir Viltevar had been turning over in the dark beneath the mausoleum, both the ward and the unmaking of every ward, now his to command. (**Mirror Image** and **Darkvision** rounded out the purchase, for a wizard does not live by locks alone.) It is the only kind of wealth a wizard truly trusts.

And then, at the day's close, the last contract - and the most interesting hand of the lot. It was **[[Gellan Primewater|Primewater]]** himself who had bid, and Primewater, being a man of subtlety, had written more into his contract than the plain eye could read. Sir Viltevar's is not a plain eye. Woven into the document in **illusory text,** visible only to one equipped to see it, ran an outline of certain *creative accountancies* - arrangements by which a greater share of the band's hard-won coin might remain in the band's own coffers, and a lesser share find its way into the kingdom's. *He knew,* Primewater remarked, with the smooth confidence of a man who has correctly read his audience, that our hero would *see* the benefit of an arrangement between them. And the noble scribe, the chronicler must report, was **genuinely flattered** - flattered as a craftsman is flattered to be recognized by another of the guild. He thanked Primewater warmly for his *vision,* pronounced it the finest offer the day had produced, and pledged to lobby the Renegades on his behalf in person - while noting, with the scruple of a man who respects the forms even when the substance delights him, that the band must render its judgment as a body.

So the day ended, and the chronicle with it. One pauses, in closing, only to admire the symmetry the day had drawn without ever once announcing it: that our hero should come to *blows* over the proper use of a smuggling road, and end the same hours *charmed* by a smuggler's ledger - and find, in his own breast, not the faintest tremor of contradiction. There is none, of course. Sir Viltevar is entirely consistent. He always has been. It is the world that keeps failing to keep up.
